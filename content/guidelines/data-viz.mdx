> Reference: [Adobe Spectrum — data visualization fundamentals](https://spectrum.adobe.com/page/data-visualization-fundamentals/). Adapted for our scale: most T&S data viz is small multiples in dashboards and gradebooks, not analyst tooling.

## Principles

- **Answer a question, don't decorate a page.** Every chart names the question it answers in its title ("Which classes have missing submissions?"), not a dataset label ("Submissions").
- **Choose the encoding by the comparison.** Position and length (bar, dot, line) before angle and area (pie, donut). If teachers must compare values, put them on a common axis.
- **Default to less.** Direct-label instead of legends where possible; gridlines light or absent; no 3D, shadows, or decorative gradients ever.

## Colour in charts

- **Categorical:** maximum 6 distinguishable hues from the shared Radix palette; beyond that, group into "other" or use small multiples.
- **Sequential/diverging:** single-hue Radix ramps for magnitude; diverging only when there's a meaningful midpoint.
- **Semantic consistency:** the same metric keeps the same colour everywhere in a product. Functional colours (success/warning/danger) keep their meaning; never use red decoratively in a chart.
- **Never colour alone:** pair with labels, patterns, or position; verify with a colour-blindness simulator (accessible-chart baseline).

## Numbers & text

- Tabular numbers in tables and axes. Locale-aware formats.
- Round to the precision teachers act on: "78%", not "78.42%".
- Empty and loading states designed: a chart with no data explains why and what to do next.

## The table question

Tables are our most-used "visualization". Density vs. core-columns is an open pattern question (OPQ-1). Until evidence closes it, both directions are valid divergence options in the harness, with column-usage telemetry attached.
